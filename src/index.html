A new way of living and learning
Peyton Smith, 15, is a freshman at Lindbergh High.

This whole pandemic has really changed my view on life, and it has shown me how fortunate I am. My mom has been working this whole time because her company is considered an essential business. Although my dad had a few weeks off from work, he went back quickly. We’ve had access to the internet, and our fridge and pantry have been able to stay fully stocked.

I was very shocked when I heard that we were going on a six-week “break” from school. I knew that COVID-19 was serious, but I didn’t realize how much more serious it was going to get. Doing school work was soon required for a grade, and I was starting to feel stressed and frustrated. I really missed the social part of school, and I wasn’t able to connect with people face to face anymore. I had to essentially create a new routine for myself.

With the school year coming to an end, I have learned to adapt to a new way of living and learning, and I will bring this experience of changing the way I lived during a global pandemic with me for the rest of my life.

Finding opportunities to share and listen
Sydney Goitia, 14, is a freshman at University Prep.

For a while I was lost about what my relationship to the pandemic was. I educated myself and took the necessary health precautions, but it took awhile for me to connect with the severity of the situation. I had anxiety about myself and the people around me becoming infected, but something about it didn’t feel real. It was hard for me to conceive that people all over the world had their focus on one thing: the coronavirus.

But eventually, it started to get to me. I started to miss the things I had taken for granted in my everyday life, like spending time with my friends and my commute across town to school. Missing these activities made it seem more real.

I realized that although I couldn’t change my situation, I could find opportunities to share my experiences and listen to others. Some projects I joined seek to help others find connection in this time of social distancing by sharing our stories through social media and film. Others involve educating and discussing the injustices some people face in our world.

If there’s anything I’ve learned from this pandemic, it’s to use your situation to your advantage and inspire others to do the same.

A different kind of 16th birthday
Ciena Sakamoto, 16, is a sophomore at Liberty High School.

This pandemic has changed everything for me. My 16th birthday was three days after the closure of school, and although seemingly unimportant, I never got the opportunity to drive myself to school. It’s the little milestones like first drives and birthdays that we are all missing out on. I miss my friends and being able to go out like a teenager.

Virtual school is not a perfect replacement and looking at a computer all day gets tough at times. I know that eventually there will be a day where we go back to school and live our normal lives again, but right now that seems pretty far away.

For me, there has been a bright side to all of this. I have been able to focus on my community work and get an internship with King County SOAR to collaborate with an amazing group of youth. This break from my busy school and social life has reminded me to slow down and check in with myself, something I rarely had time for. But most important, I have learned the importance of community and adaptation to unexpected possibilities. Going into the future, I know that life has challenges but if I look in the right places, opportunities will always arise.

Forming habits, pulling myself through
Ruby Lee, 15, is a freshman at Rainier Beach High School.

Freshman year was supposed to be the time I got comfortable and established a relationship with the school by getting to know teachers and classmates. A time to find my place there. But as of March, I was stuck at home, the days just rolling by.

At first, I treated it all like a long break. I slacked on homework, stayed up late and procrastinated. I didn’t really realize how big of an effect this was having on my mental health. My whole world was flipped. I was more emotional, I would get stressed easier, even just tired more often.

Eventually, I accepted what was happening, forgot about all the thoughts and feelings about why it sucked, and took action. Making tea, settling into my desk seat and starting work became my daily habit. This was helpful because it told my body, “OK now we’re in work mode” and it set the tone for the entire day.

Of course I still sometimes get emotional or sad, but I’ve learned what my body needs to cope. Maybe I need to get out of the house, or maybe I need to talk to someone I love. I’m grateful for what this quarantine has shown me about myself and the tiny details that make life worth living.

Making lunch
TJ Joseph, 16, is a junior at Garfield High School.

School has been significantly easier for me since the pandemic started. I have a lot less homework, and my time is much easier to manage. I am learning things at a much slower rate, which feels right for adjusting to online school. My teachers are very understanding and flexible with work.

I now have time to do things that I couldn’t do during the school year, like sleeping in, making lunch for myself and spending more time with my family.

During the school year, I’d only be around my family for about an hour each day due to our schedules, but now I get to see them throughout the day. It’s been better for my individual relationships, especially with my dad who I saw the least when he had work.

I have enjoyed online school, but I will not be sad when it comes to an end. It is a good temporary fix, but seeing my friends and interacting with my teachers in person allows me to learn better and be a better student.

One thing I can take from online schooling is finding how to balance my time better. After high school, I won’t have to sit in school for seven hours every day, and being in quarantine has allowed me to create a schedule that I can use in the future to be the most productive version of myself.
